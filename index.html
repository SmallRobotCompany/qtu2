01

Wilma is LIVE

Wilma launched to commercial customers

Wilma is live! Live per plant data is now being processed through Wilma for customers. This is a significant milestone in our mission to Per Plant Farming. We’ve had the ability to gather per plant data for a while, but October 2020 was the first time that we were able to demonstrate live Per Plant data in Wilma from customer farms in the UK.

The brains behind our team of agri-robots launched at REAP 2020; the annual conference for the UK agritech network Agri-TechE.

Press release Wilma Blog Farmers Weekly Crop Production Magazine
Billions of data points

It’s taken until now because wheat (our first target crop) is generally planted (“drilled”) in the autumn in the UK. We’re now approaching billions of data points per farm - a level of detail that we believe far surpasses anything else being gathered on farms anywhere in the world - with a focus on a per plant weed count, and a per plant crop count.

We are processing this through our custom built, production ready software platform - Wilma - and now working with farmers to action the insights that we have been able to produce. The objective is to use this data to reduce herbicide usage on farms and to help farmers to improve the timing of their fertiliser applications.   

Why it’s significant?

Farming is an industry which varies enormously from region to region. A farm in Gujarat operates in a very different way to a farm in Hampshire, and yet they both share a goal of producing food. Therefore, whilst there are many aspects of what we are doing which will need to be adapted for local environments, the ability to gather per plant data is a capability which is scalable globally.

Our vision is that any farm of any size, growing any crop, anywhere in the world will be able to manage their crops on a per plant basis. This capability developed by Small Robot Company is a keystone to enable that future to be built. 

02

Development of our robot handler interface

Real time updates

We have developed a tablet based interface for our robot handler team to interact with the robots. This provides them with real time updates on the status of the robot, gives them a view of what the robot is looking at and enables them to remotely take control of the machine if necessary. It can be run on any device and is being used on live customer sites at the moment.

Watch video

Why it’s significant

This is the first step towards autonomy. The ability to operate robots remotely is key to us being able to scale our service across multiple sites and multiple countries. Although we haven’t tested this yet, we now in theory have the ability to remotely control and interact with our robots from the other side of the planet. Longer term, it will also be possible to provide training for farm managers and agronomists to interact with the robots which provides a further path towards accelerating our ability to scale our Farming as a Service business model.

03

 Precision placement
of a robot deployed probe

Twice as accurate as the best systems used commercially on farms today. 

Last month, we carried out successful trials demonstrating our ability to hit a predefined 10mm target with a probe attached to our prototype weed killing robot. This is twice as accurate as the best systems used commercially on farms today. Fairly soon, by combining GPS and a custom computer vision approach, we will be able to demonstrate a system which is even more accurate than this in field.

An end to end non-chemical
weed killing service

Developing the ability for a robot to be able to interact with a crop on a per plant basis is central to what we are trying to achieve and essential for our vision of a 4th Agricultural Revolution led by Per Plant farming to become a reality.

The trial that we carried out this month is a dry run for our first demonstration of an end to end non-chemical weed killing service - being able to detect a single weed in a field, place that data point on a map and then have a second robot move to that data point and take an action (in this instance, killing the weed with an electrical probe). Our target is to be able to demonstrate this live in the field in the spring

Scalable globally across any farm

Ultimately, as mentioned in previous updates, our ambition would be to have this operation carried out by a single robot - but this is the most economical and scalable way to achieve our aims at present.

Again, this is a core capability which is scalable globally across any farm of any size, regardless of the crop being grown.

It is also a key proof point leading towards our ambition of becoming a hardware platform. Although our first use of this capability is to fit an electrical probe for a per plant, non-chemical weeding service, there is the potential to fit other sensors, probes or applications to our robot so that other technologies can be deployed in a much more accurate way. This will provide a much better route to market for technologies which are currentlyunder utilised, and will become an additional revenue stream for Small Robot Company.

Competitor news: X

We've been talking to the X team at Google about the future of Ag Tech since the beginning of this year, so we were expecting their announcement about crop-inspecting robots.

This is validation for our focus on Per Plant Farming - Google very much see the same opportunity that we do, which provides an element of confirmation that our overall strategy is taking us in the right direction.

We are of course keeping a careful eye on all potential competitors, and view Google as a potential threat if they decide to get into ag hardware. However, our biggest threat is not a tech company, a startup, or even an ag machinery incumbent. It is farmers sticking with what they have. We have a long journey to show real benefits for the majority of farmers. It is exciting. The light in the eyes of farmers once they 'get it' is such a thrill. Our pure focus right now is proving the vision.
